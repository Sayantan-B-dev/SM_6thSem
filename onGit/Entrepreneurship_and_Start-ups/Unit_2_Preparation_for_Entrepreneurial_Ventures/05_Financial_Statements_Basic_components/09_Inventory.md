## 1. Definition

Inventory refers to the goods and materials that a business holds for the ultimate purpose of resale, production, or consumption. In financial statements, inventory is a current asset that includes raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods ready for sale.

## 2. Concept Explanation

Every business that sells products must keep a certain stock of items to ensure smooth operations. This stock is called inventory. The basic idea is to have the right quantity of goods available at the right time to meet customer demand without unnecessary delay.

How it works: A business buys or produces items in advance of sale. Raw materials are stored until they are sent for production. Semi-finished goods are held at various stages. Finished goods wait in the warehouse until shipped to customers. All these goods are collectively called inventory and are recorded as a current asset on the balance sheet because they are expected to be converted into cash within the operating cycle.

Why it is important: Inventory is often the largest current asset for manufacturing and trading firms. It directly affects the cost of goods sold, gross profit, and net income. Managing inventory efficiently is critical because holding too much stock ties up cash and increases storage costs, while holding too little can lead to stockouts and lost sales. The valuation method chosen also impacts reported profits and tax liability.

## 3. Key Characteristics / Features

- **Tangible Asset:** Inventory consists of physical items that can be seen, counted, and measured.
- **Current Asset Classification:** It is expected to be sold or used within one normal business cycle or twelve months.
- **Directly Linked to Core Operations:** Inventory is essential for generating revenue; without it, a trading or manufacturing firm cannot operate.
- **Dynamic Nature:** The level of inventory changes constantly with purchases, production, sales, and returns.
- **Valuation Requirement:** Inventory must be valued at cost or net realisable value, whichever is lower, as per accounting principles.

## 4. Types / Classification

Inventory is classified into three main types based on its stage in the production cycle:

- **Raw Materials:** Basic materials and components that have not yet entered the production process. For a furniture maker, wood logs, nails, and varnish are raw materials.
- **Work-in-Progress (WIP):** Goods that are partially completed in the manufacturing process. They have incurred some labour and overhead costs but are not yet ready for sale. For example, a chair that is assembled but not polished is WIP.
- **Finished Goods:** Products that are completely manufactured, inspected, and ready to be sold to customers. For a bakery, bread packed and ready for delivery is finished goods inventory.

For a trading business (retailer/wholesaler), inventory consists only of "merchandise inventory" — finished goods purchased for resale without further processing.

## 5. Working / Mechanism

The life cycle of inventory flows through the business in a systematic way:

1.  The business purchases raw materials from suppliers. This adds to the raw materials inventory.
2.  When production begins, raw materials are taken out of the store, and after processing, they become work-in-progress inventory.
3.  As the goods pass through final assembly and quality checks, they are transferred to finished goods inventory.
4.  When a sale occurs, finished goods are shipped to the customer.
5.  In the accounting books, the cost of these sold goods is moved from the inventory account (asset) to the cost of goods sold (expense) on the income statement.
6.  At the end of the accounting period, any unsold units remain as closing inventory, reported as a current asset.
7.  This closing inventory becomes the opening inventory for the next period, continuing the cycle.

## 7. Mathematical Formulation

The value of closing inventory directly affects the Cost of Goods Sold (COGS):

$$
\text{COGS} = \text{Opening Inventory} + \text{Purchases} + \text{Direct Expenses} - \text{Closing Inventory}
$$

Where:
- **Opening Inventory** = Value of inventory at the beginning of the period.
- **Purchases** = Net cost of new raw materials or goods bought during the period.
- **Direct Expenses** = Costs like freight and factory wages directly traceable to production.
- **Closing Inventory** = Value of inventory remaining at the end of the period.

A higher closing inventory results in lower COGS, which increases gross profit.

Inventory Turnover Ratio measures how efficiently inventory is managed:

$$
\text{Inventory Turnover Ratio} = \frac{\text{Cost of Goods Sold}}{\text{Average Inventory}}
$$

Where Average Inventory = (Opening Inventory + Closing Inventory) / 2. A higher ratio indicates faster movement and efficient management.

## 8. Example

Riya runs a small textile unit. At the start of April, her inventory records show:
- Opening stock: ₹50,000 (raw material and finished cloth).
- Purchases of yarn and dyes during April: ₹1,20,000.
- Direct labour and power: ₹30,000.
- At the end of April, unsold stock (closing inventory) valued at ₹40,000.

COGS for April = 50,000 + 1,20,000 + 30,000 – 40,000 = ₹1,60,000.
The closing inventory of ₹40,000 will be shown as a current asset on the balance sheet for April. It will also be the opening stock for May.

## 9. Analogy

Think of a refrigerator in a household kitchen. The vegetables, milk, and eggs you buy are like raw materials. A half-cooked curry on the stove is work-in-progress. The prepared dish on the dining table is finished goods. You don’t want an empty fridge, or you will go hungry. But overstocking leads to spoilage and waste. Inventory in a business works the same way: enough to meet demand, not too much to block money and space.

## 11. Advantages

- Ensures uninterrupted production by keeping raw materials readily available.
- Enables immediate order fulfillment, leading to customer satisfaction and repeat business.
- Acts as a buffer against price fluctuations and supply chain disruptions.
- Efficient inventory management reduces holding costs and frees up cash for other uses.
- Accurate inventory valuation is essential for correct profit measurement and tax calculation.

## 12. Disadvantages / Limitations

- Excess inventory blocks working capital and increases storage, insurance, and handling costs.
- Risk of obsolescence, theft, or spoilage, especially for perishable or fashion items.
- Too little inventory can result in stockouts, lost sales, and damage to business reputation.
- Inventory valuation can be complex due to different methods (FIFO, LIFO, Weighted Average), affecting profit comparability.
- Physical verification and record-keeping require time, personnel, and robust systems.

## 13. Important Points / Exam Notes

- Inventory is classified as a current asset because it is expected to be sold or used within one operating cycle.
- The three major types are raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods.
- Closing inventory is valued at the lower of cost or net realisable value (conservatism principle).
- COGS = Opening Inventory + Purchases + Direct Expenses – Closing Inventory.
- Inventory Turnover Ratio = COGS / Average Inventory; a high ratio indicates efficient inventory management.
- Common inventory valuation methods: FIFO (First-In-First-Out), LIFO (Last-In-First-Out), and Weighted Average Cost.

## 14. Applications / Use Cases

- **Retail Store Management:** A supermarket tracks inventory levels daily to reorder fast-moving items and discount slow-moving ones.
- **Manufacturing Planning:** An automobile plant maintains just-in-time (JIT) inventory to reduce holding costs while ensuring the assembly line never stops.
- **Loan Appraisal:** Banks examine inventory turnover ratios and stock levels to evaluate working capital efficiency before sanctioning loans.
- **Financial Analysis:** Investors compare inventory days of a company with industry peers to gauge operational efficiency.
- **Audit and Compliance:** Physical stock-taking at year-end ensures that the inventory figure in the balance sheet is reliable and free from fraud.

## 15. MCQs

**Q1. Which of the following is classified as work-in-progress inventory for a car manufacturer?**

A. Tires purchased but not yet fitted  
B. A fully assembled car ready for sale  
C. A car body painted but without an engine fitted  
D. Office furniture used by the finance team  
**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** A car that is partially complete (painted but missing engine) is work-in-progress.

**Q2. Inventory is shown in the balance sheet as a:**

A. Long-term liability  
B. Current asset  
C. Fixed asset  
D. Operating expense  
**Answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Inventory is expected to be sold within the next operating cycle, so it is a current asset.

**Q3. The cost of goods sold formula is:**

A. Opening Inventory + Purchases + Closing Inventory  
B. Opening Inventory + Purchases – Closing Inventory  
C. Purchases – Closing Inventory  
D. Sales – Opening Inventory  
**Answer:** B  
**Explanation:** COGS = Opening Inventory + Purchases + Direct Expenses – Closing Inventory.

**Q4. Which inventory valuation method is based on the principle that the oldest stock is sold first?**

A. LIFO  
B. Weighted average  
C. FIFO  
D. Standard cost  
**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** FIFO assumes goods purchased first are sold first; remaining inventory consists of the latest purchases.

**Q5. A high Inventory Turnover Ratio indicates:**

A. Inventory is moving slowly  
B. Large quantities of dead stock  
C. Efficient inventory management and fast sales  
D. High holding costs  
**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** High turnover means the company sells and replaces its inventory quickly, reducing holding costs.

**Q6. If a fire destroys a portion of raw material stock, this loss will primarily affect:**

A. Fixed assets  
B. Work-in-progress directly  
C. Raw material inventory value and potential future production  
D. Finished goods turnover  
**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** The destruction reduces raw material inventory, leading to a loss and possible disruption.

**Q7. Which of the following is NOT a type of manufacturing inventory?**

A. Raw materials  
B. Work-in-progress  
C. Merchandise inventory (for the manufacturer)  
D. Finished goods  
**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** Merchandise inventory is the term used for trading businesses; manufacturers have raw materials, WIP, and finished goods.

**Q8. Closing inventory is valued at:**

A. Maximum retail price  
B. Market selling price irrespective of cost  
C. Cost or net realisable value, whichever is lower  
D. Always at original purchase cost  
**Answer:** C  
**Explanation:** Accounting standards require the lower of cost and net realisable value for prudency.

**Q9. The inventory level at the end of one period becomes the __________ at the beginning of the next period.**

A. Closing stock  
B. Opening stock  
C. Purchases  
D. Sales  
**Answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Closing inventory of one year is carried forward as opening inventory of the next.

**Q10. Excessive inventory holding can lead to which problem?**

A. Stockouts and lost sales  
B. Increased holding costs and risk of obsolescence  
C. Lower working capital requirements  
D. Better cash flow  
**Answer:** B  
**Explanation:** Overstocking ties up cash and increases storage, insurance, and obsolescence costs.
