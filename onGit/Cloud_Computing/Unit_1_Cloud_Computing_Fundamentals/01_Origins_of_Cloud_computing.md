# Origins of Cloud computing

The origins of cloud computing are a fascinating story, evolving over more than half a century. It's not the invention of any single person or company, but rather the convergence of several key ideas: treating computing as a utility, developing technologies like virtualisation and time-sharing, and the growth of the internet. Here is a detailed look at that evolution.

### The Seeds of Centralized Computing (1950s-1960s)
The earliest concepts were born out of necessity when computers were room-sized, enormously expensive, and scarce.

*   **The Vision of Computing as a Utility**: In the early 1950s, several visionaries imagined computing power being accessed like a public utility, much like electricity or water. Key figures included **Herb Grosch**, **John McCarthy**, and **Douglas F. Parkhill**. In his 1966 book *The Challenge of the Computer Utility*, Parkhill predicted the computer industry would evolve into a public utility with many remote users connected to a central facility.

*   **The First "Intergalactic" Network**: In the 1960s, **J.C.R. Licklider**, a pioneer at ARPA, envisioned a global network connecting people and data from anywhere. His work on ARPANET and his memos about an "Intergalactic Computer Network" foresaw a future that closely resembles the modern internet and cloud.

*   **Time-Sharing: The Precursor to Multi-Tenancy**: The practical foundation for cloud computing was the concept of **time-sharing**, first proposed by John McCarthy in 1955. As computers were too expensive for any single user to own, companies like IBM offered "service bureaus" in the 1960s and 70s. This allowed multiple users to rent time on a single mainframe using their own terminals, effectively sharing the machine's processing power without having to buy one outright.

### Key Technologies that Changed Everything (1960s-1980s)
Several foundational technologies emerged during this period, enabling the move toward more abstract and shared computing resources.

*   **Virtualisation: The Foundation of Cloud Architecture**: In 1959, British computer scientist **Christopher Strachey** formally introduced the concept of **virtualisation** in his paper "Time Sharing in Large Fast Computer". This technology allows a single physical server to be partitioned into multiple isolated virtual machines, each running its own operating system and applications. This is a core technology that allows cloud providers to offer efficient, scalable services. IBM later implemented this by virtualising operating systems in 1967, enabling multiple users to share the same resources.

*   **The Birth of the Internet**: The launch of **ARPANET** in 1969, the network that evolved into the modern internet, was a crucial step in providing the connectivity that cloud computing relies on.

*   **Networking Takes Shape**: The development of local area networks (LANs) in the 1970s and technologies like Sun Microsystems' Network File System (NFS) protocol in 1984 allowed users to access files over a network, making the idea of remote data storage more practical.

### From Concept to Commercial Reality (1990s - Early 2000s)
By the 1990s, the commercial internet arrived, and companies began experimenting with delivering applications and services over the web.

*   **Early Internet Services**: In 1983, **CompuServe** offered its users a small amount of disk space for file storage, an early form of online storage.
*   **The "Cloud" Symbol**: The use of a stylized cloud as a network diagram symbol became common in this era, which many believe is the origin of the term's visual reference.
*   **Software-as-a-Service (SaaS) Pioneers**: The late 1990s saw the rise of **Application Service Providers (ASPs)**, which delivered software applications over the internet. Although many early ASPs failed, they paved the way for the SaaS model. In 1999, **Salesforce** launched, becoming the first major success story for delivering enterprise applications via a simple website. It pioneered the SaaS model and proved that software could be delivered and paid for as a subscription.

### The Modern Cloud Takes Shape (2006-Present)
The modern cloud computing era began in 2006 when the infrastructure needed to be both powerful and user-friendly finally aligned.

*   **Amazon Web Services (AWS)**: In March 2006, Amazon launched **Amazon Simple Storage Service (S3)**, solving a major problem for startups: where to store data without massive upfront costs. It was soon followed by **Elastic Compute Cloud (EC2)**, which offered scalable, on-demand computing power (Infrastructure-as-a-Service, or IaaS). Amazon's genius was recognizing that the internal infrastructure they had built to scale their e-commerce business could be rented out to others.
*   **The Expansion**: Following AWS's lead, **Google** launched its App Engine platform (PaaS) in 2008, and **Microsoft** launched its Azure cloud platform in 2010. This competition solidified the cloud market and accelerated innovation.

### How the Term "Cloud Computing" Was Coined
The origin of the term itself is a popular topic with a few different claims.

*   **The Academic Definition (1997)**: Professor **Ramnath K. Chellappa** gave the first academic definition in 1997, describing it as a "computing paradigm where the boundaries of computing will be determined by economic rationale rather than technical limits alone".
*   **Internal Corporate Use (1996)**: An investigation by *MIT Technology Review* suggests the phrase was first used in 1996 by a group of **Compaq** executives (Sean O'Sullivan and George Favaloro) in an internal analysis about the future of internet-based business.
*   **Mainstream Popularity (2006)**: The term exploded into the mainstream in 2006 when **Eric Schmidt**, then CEO of Google, used it during a conference to describe Google's approach to software services, describing an emergent new model where data and services live "in a cloud somewhere".

In summary, the cloud wasn't built in a day. It's the culmination of decades of visionary ideas, from John McCarthy's time-sharing theory to the commercial launch of AWS, each step building on the last to create a technology that now powers much of our daily lives.
